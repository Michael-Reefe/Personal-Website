This demo shows what happens to a theoretical parcel of gas that starts out with a density of 0.1 particles cm-3 and a temperature of 108 Kelvin, and then cools down over time. The models shown have been computed with the state-of-the-art CLOUDY code.

The top-left plot shows the cooling function, which tells us how quickly the gas is able to cool at a given temperature. So you can see that as time evolves, the highlighted point moves to the left (lower temperatures) as the gas cools down. You'll notice a general trend that the gas can cool more quickly at lower temperatures (since the cooling function is higher). This is because the primary cooling mechanism transitions from bremsstrahlung emission to line emission.

In actuality, what is plotted is the cooling rate divided by the density squared. And if we assume an ideal gas law where density is proportional to the inverse of temperature, then the trend of the cooling rate getting faster at lower temperatures is exaggerated even further. You'll notice this if you look at how quickly the time value changes in the top-right plot. Initially, when we are at very hot temperatures, the time value changes very quickly, indicating that it takes a very long time for the gas to cool. Whereas, once we get below about 1 million Kelvin, the time value starts changing much more slowly, indicating that the gas is cooling very rapidly!

The top-right plot, in addition to showing the current temperature and time in the simulation, shows a mock 2D image of what cooling line emission in a diffuse gas cloud might look like. Since we are focusing on one cooling emission line, and these lines tend to operate at a relatively narrow temperature range, this image will be blank most of the time, and then it will suddenly flash brightly once the gas reaches the temperature that is ideal for the cooling line we are looking at. You can change which cooling line this is in the control panel. The 2D surface brightness distribution uses a simple physically motivated beta model.

Finally, the bottom plot shows the real-time observed spectrum of the gas cloud, with continuum emission and emission lines. This is an idealized model where there are no stars and no dust, so it shows just the emission that would be expected from a cooling region of gas. Some strong cooling lines are highlighted, and you will see as time evolves that these lines will become bright when the conditions of the gas are favorable for their existence (right density and temperature). For most of the lines considered here, this will be somewhere in the 105-106 Kelvin range.

Below the plots, there are some options that you can play with to explore different physical conditions!

  • You can swap between isobaric (constant pressure) and isochoric (constant density) cooling. Note that this wont affect the shape of the cooling function, because it is normalized by the density squared!
  • You can swap between collisional ionization equilibrium (CIE) and non-equilibrium ionization (NEI). The difference here is based on whether the ionization state of the gas is forced to reach equilibrium at each timestep.
  • You can change the metallicity, which affects how many metals are present in the gas (where "metals" means any element other than hydrogen or helium).
  • You can choose between a set of cooling emission lines to focus on in the spectrum. This also affects which brightness is shown in the mock 2D image.
