Extrasolar and Extragalactic

From planets around other stars (exoplanets) to the most energetic black holes in the universe, my research has focused on observational and numerical methods to finding and better understanding these objects. Learn more about each project below.

Coronal Emission Lines

The hot, dense regions surrounding the accretion disks of Active Galactic Nuclei (AGNs) can sometimes produce high ionization-potential coronal emission lines (CLs) through collisional excitations, and subsequent radiative de-excitations, which can then be observed assuming the coronal line region is rarefied enough below the critical density of the CL in question.

I conducted a wide-scale survey of almost 1 million optical SDSS spectra in search of these CLs. Aside from the obvious conclusion that CL emission is rare, showing up in less than 0.02% of galaxies, we found that CL emission preferrentially shows up in lower mass galaxies compared to those identified with BPT ratios and WISE colors, potentially revealing a whole new population of dwarf galaxy AGNs.

Exoplanet Validations

Each exoplanet candidate from NASA's TESS mission (and previously Kepler) requires a rigorous follow-up validation process with additional transit observations to eliminate nearby eclipsing binary scenarios, and radial velocities (RVs) to get a planet mass estimate. When a planet orbits a star, its gravitational force induces a small "wobble" in the star around the barycenter of the system. The radial velocity method takes advantage of the Doppler shift induced by this wobble to get a measurement of the planet's mass.

We performed RV observations and analysis of the TOI 620 system, confirming a single planet, and the AU Mic system, with 2 planets. AU Mic in particular is interesting because it is a very young star (~20 Myr), so any planets around it could provide valuable insights about how planets form and evolve in a debris disk. But young stars are also very active spectroscopically, which makes their RVs very noisy and difficult to analyze. I worked with a graduate student, who developed a chromatic Gaussian Process (GP) kernel to help separate stellar activity from Keplerian planet signals, to analyze the AU Mic system and validate the 2 planets in it.

Telescope Automation

The large volume of exoplanet candidates coming in from TESS, each with a need to be validated, drives a need for automated follow-up systems. I led an effort to fully automate exoplanet TESS transit follow-up observations with the George Mason University 0.8-meter campus telescope. The automation code was written in Python and developed in a fully asynchronous, multithreaded framework, which allowed us to maximize the efficiency of observations by reducing overhead and down-time between hardware operations like slewing the telescope and opening the dome shutter.

The code is autonomous enough to run for multiple days or even weeks at a time, and is even able to focus and guide on the target and automatically collect calibration images (darks and flats). But most importantly for a telescope in the humid climate of the East Coast, an automated weather monitor constantly checks the current conditions for high humidity, winds, precipitation, and cloud coverage, and causes the observatory to close up and pause operations during inclement weather. This allows us to be confident in the safety of the automation code and save some nights of sleep. As of 3/30/2022, the code has been used on 170 observing nights and counting!
