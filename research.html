Galaxy Clusters and the Cooling Flow Problem

Galaxy clusters are collections of 100s-1000s of galaxies and are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe. But the stars and galaxies in the cluster only contain a small fraction of the cluster's luminous mass---less than 10%. The other 90% is attributed to the gas and dust between the galaxies in the cluster, called the intracluster medium (ICM). In general, the ICM is incredibly hot (10 million Kelvin) and rarefied (less than 1 particle per cubic centimeter), which means it should not be able to cool off efficiently. However, the ICM tends to get cooler and more dense towards the center of the cluster, and in some clusters where this effect is pronounced, we should expect to see the gas cooling down, condensing, and fueling star formation in the central brightest cluster galaxy within observable timescales. Nevertheless, what we actually observe is that the gas does begin to cool out of the hot X-ray emitting phase, but it never makes it to the cold molecular phase to fuel star formation. Evidently, at some point, the gas either vanishes completely, or is being reheated by some mechanism. This is known as the cooling flow problem.

Coronal Emission Lines and Integral Field Unit Spectroscopy

My current research focuses on studying the ICM with integral field spectroscopy in galaxy clusters, with the aim of better understanding the cooling flow problem, and how feedback from an active galactic nucleus (AGN) plays a role in reheating the gas. Integral field spectroscopy is revolutionary in that it enables us to obtain spatial and spectroscopic information simultaneously. We get an entire 3D "cube" of data, where each pixel in a flat 2D image contains an entire spectrum. I have been targeting coronal emission lines, which probe gas at intermediate temperatures (100,000s of Kelvin), to reveal how the gas cools from the hot X-ray phase to the cold star-forming phase, using world-class instrumentation with NASA's JWST mission in the infrared, as well as other instruments in the UV and optical.
